01 — Introduction

Why Is Literature Searching Challenging?

Literature searching is an essential aspect of all research. Most resources are now electronically available — yet this doesn't make retrieval easier. The challenge is not finding answers, it's finding the right ones.

🌊

Volume Problem

Google can return 100,000 results for any query. Without a strategy, sifting through them is an impossible task. You need a librarian's precision, not a search engine's breadth.

🎯

Precision vs Recall

Too broad → irrelevant noise. Too narrow → miss important papers. The goal is a search that is sensitive enough to capture all relevant work, yet specific enough to avoid useless hits.

🗣️

Vocabulary Problem

Different authors use different words for the same concept. Without controlled vocabulary (like MeSH), searches miss synonyms and alternative terms used across the literature.

📅

Currency

Science moves fast. A search must capture the most up-to-date literature, which requires databases that are frequently updated and indexed promptly after publication.

02 — Literature Resources

Popular Literature Databases

Google Scholar

Specialised Google search engine for academic literature across all disciplines. Broad coverage but limited filtering capabilities compared to curated databases.

PubMed

Bibliographical database run by the NCBI covering a wide range of biomedical literature including MEDLINE. Free, highly curated, and searchable with advanced syntax.

PubMed Clinical Queries

A specialised PubMed search interface optimised for searching clinical data — filters by study type, clinical study category, and medical genetics.

Scopus

The largest abstract and citation database of peer-reviewed literature across all scientific domains. Useful for citation tracking and author/journal metrics.

ScienceDirect

Linked to Scopus and Elsevier journals. Allows full-text searching across thousands of peer-reviewed journals from all scientific domains.

Web of Science / Embase

Other major commercial databases that license MEDLINE and provide additional coverage and citation analysis tools beyond PubMed's free interface.

03 — MEDLINE & PubMed

MEDLINE, PubMed — What's the Difference?

🏛️

MEDLINE

Produced by the US National Library of Medicine (NLM) — the world's largest medical library. NLM has been indexing biomedical literature since 1897. The printed index (Index Medicus) was converted into the database known today as MEDLINE, which now holds over 24 million citations.

🔍

PubMed

PubMed (Public MEDLINE) is NLM's free search engine interface for MEDLINE. Since 1996, free access to MEDLINE has been made available to the public via PubMed at NCBI. PubMed also contains citations from additional biomedical journals and books beyond MEDLINE, giving access to over 30 million citations.

Why use PubMed / MEDLINE?
✅

Comprehensive & Current

Up-to-date information from credible, peer-reviewed biomedical sources across all life science fields.

🗂️

Controlled Vocabulary

MeSH terms ensure consistent indexing — searches retrieve articles regardless of the exact synonym used by the author.

📄

Abstract & Full Text

Abstracts always available; many articles link to free full text via PubMed Central (PMC) or publisher sites.

🔧

Advanced Filtering

Filter by article type, date, species, language, sex, age group, and text availability to narrow your results efficiently.

04 — Search Strategy

Literature Search Steps

Using the FOXP2 example from the slides: studying genetic variation in FOXP2 and its impact on speech development disorders.

Clearly define your question

Know exactly what information you are looking for before you start. e.g. "mutations in FOXP2 linked to speech development disorders."

Break into keywords

Decompose your question: FOXP2 + speech development + mutation. Each is searched independently first.

Identify quality sources

Choose a single high-quality resource like PubMed rather than mixing search engines that return inconsistent quality.

Combine with operators

Use AND, OR, NOT to combine your keywords logically. Build up the query in the Advanced Search Builder step by step.

Apply field tags

Restrict terms to specific fields (Title, Abstract, Author) to improve precision. e.g. foxp2[Title/Abstract].

Refine with MeSH

Look up MeSH terms for your keywords to find the controlled vocabulary and include the official term in your search.

Filter results

Apply PubMed filters: article type, date range, free full text, species, publication year to narrow to what's relevant.

Save & export

Save results to clipboard, My Bibliography, Collections, or export to a Citation Manager (e.g., Zotero, EndNote).

💡 FOXP2 Search Example:
Simple search "foxp2" → 604 results  |  AND "speech development" → 30,109 results for that term  |  AND "mutation" → 1,093,123 results for that term  |  Combined: ((foxp2) AND (speech development)) AND (mutation) → 92 results. Adding [Title/Abstract] tags → 3 highly specific results.

AND — Narrows Search

Retrieves records containing all the search terms. FOXP2 alone = 604. FOXP2 AND speech = 285. Fewer but more relevant results.

OR — Broadens Search

Retrieves records containing any of the search terms. Useful for synonyms. FOXP2 OR speech = 124,578. Far more results covering either concept.

NOT — Excludes Terms

Removes records containing the excluded term. FOXP2 NOT speech = 319. Dangerous to overuse — can accidentally exclude relevant papers.

Other search operators
✂️

Truncation — Wildcard *

Child* retrieves Child, Children, Childhood, Childlike. The asterisk (*) stands for any characters. Great for plurals and variant endings.

💬

Phrase Search — Quotation Marks

"copy number variation" searches for the exact phrase. Without quotes, PubMed may search each word independently and combine with AND.

06 — Field Tags

Restrict Searches with Field Tags

PubMed field tags restrict a search term to a specific metadata field. Written in square brackets: term[TAG]. The Advanced Search Builder generates these automatically from its dropdown menus.

Field Name[TAG]What It Searches
All Fields[ALL]All searchable fields in the record (default)
Title[TI]Article title only
Title or Abstract[TIAB]Words in the article title or its abstract
Author[AU]Author name (e.g., Lai CS[AU])
Last Author[LASTAU]Last personal author in the citation
Full Author Name[FAU]Full author name (2002 onward)
Journal Title[TA]Journal name, abbreviation, or ISSN
Publication Date[PD]Date article was published (e.g. 2001[PD])
Publication Type[PT]Format: letter, clinical trial, review, etc.
MeSH Major Topic[MAJR]Main MeSH topic of an article
MeSH Terms[MH]Any assigned MeSH term
MeSH Subheading[SH]Chemical or substance subheading
Text Word[TW]All textual fields of the PubMed record
Substance Name[NM]Substance names discussed in the article
Language[LA]Language of the article (not the abstract)
Year[DP]Year published — e.g. 2000:2009[DP] for range
Affiliation[AD]First author's institutional affiliation
Pagination[PG]First page number of the article
🔬 FOXP2 Precision Example: Searching foxp2[Title/Abstract] → 555 results. Adding AND (speech development[Title/Abstract]) AND (mutation[Title/Abstract]) → 3 results. Then replacing "speech development" with the correct MeSH term "language development disorders[MH]" → 13 highly specific results including the original landmark Lai et al. 2001 paper.
07 — MeSH Terms

Medical Subject Headings — MeSH

MeSH is a controlled vocabulary — a set of standardised terms used by the NLM to index MEDLINE articles. Subject specialists read every article and assign the appropriate MeSH terms to it.

📖

What is MeSH?

Medical Subject Headings — a hierarchical taxonomy maintained by NLM. It ensures that articles about the same topic are grouped together, regardless of the exact terminology each author used.

👩‍⚕️

How Articles Get MeSH Terms

NLM subject specialists read each article and assign the most appropriate MeSH terms based on the article's content — not just its keywords. This is manual curation at scale.

🌳

Hierarchical Structure

"Speech development" in MeSH → Diseases → Nervous System → Neurologic Manifestations → Communication Disorders → Language Disorders → Language Development Disorders → Specific Language Disorder.

🎯

Why Use MeSH?

Searching "speech development" as free text misses articles where the author wrote "language development disorders" or "verbal communication delay." MeSH catches them all with one term.

💡 MeSH Search Tip: Before searching PubMed, visit the MeSH Browser (mesh.nlm.nih.gov) and look up your keyword. The browser shows you the correct MeSH term, its hierarchy, and related terms. Then use that official term with the [MH] tag in your PubMed query for maximum precision.
08 — Summary

What You Should Remember

  • Literature searching is less frustrating when you select a single high-quality resource like PubMed.
  • Break your research question into individual keywords and search each one first to understand result counts.
  • Use AND to narrow, OR to broaden, and NOT to exclude — building your query step by step in the Advanced Search Builder.
  • Use quotation marks for exact phrases and asterisk (*) truncation for variant word endings.
  • Field tags ([TI], [TIAB], [AU], [DP]) restrict a term to a specific metadata field and dramatically improve precision.
  • MeSH terms are controlled vocabulary assigned by NLM specialists — always look up the official MeSH term for your keywords.
  • PubMed's advanced search history lets you combine prior searches by their number (e.g., #1 AND #2 AND #3).
  • Use PubMed filters (article type, date, free full text) and display options to refine your final result set.
