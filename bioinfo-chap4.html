01 — Introduction

What Is Sequence Alignment and Why Do It?

Sequence alignment is a way of arranging two or more sequences of DNA, RNA, or protein to identify regions of similarity. Comparison is one of the most effective methods for making discoveries in science — and sequence alignment is the most common comparative method in molecular biology.

🔮

Predict Function

A newly sequenced gene with high similarity to a known gene likely shares the same biological function.

🔗

Merge Short Reads

Short DNA fragments from high-throughput sequencing are merged into longer contigs by finding overlapping regions.

🏷️

Auto-annotate Genomes

Newly sequenced genomes are annotated by aligning their sequences to known, annotated reference genomes.

🌳

Phylogenetic Analysis

Sequence alignment is the first and essential step in constructing phylogenetic trees to study evolutionary relationships.

🏗️

Structure Prediction

Essential for molecular structure prediction — structurally similar proteins often have similar sequences.

🧪

Detect Mutations

Identify substitutions, insertions, and deletions by comparing a sample sequence to a reference sequence.

Pairwise Alignment

Comparing exactly two sequences. The fundamental unit of bioinformatics — used in BLAST, database searching, and variant calling.

Multiple Sequence Alignment (MSA)

Comparing more than 2 sequences simultaneously. Used for phylogeny, conserved region identification, and protein family analysis.

02 — Similarity & Identity

Sequence Identity vs Similarity

Similarity and identity measure how alike two sequences are. For nucleotide sequences, they are synonymous. For protein sequences, similarity can be higher than identity because different amino acids may have similar chemical properties.

Seq 3: AGCTGGCATTATGGATGGCTG
Seq 4: AGCTGACATTATCGTATGGCTG
→ 90% identity / 90% similarity — 2 point mutations

Formula — Equal Length Sequences

Percentage similarity/identity S or I:

S = [2L / (L_y + L_z)] × 100

Where L = number of aligned residues with similar/identical characteristics, L_y and L_z = lengths of the two sequences.

Formula — Unequal Length Sequences

When sequences have different lengths:

I(S) = (L_i(s) / L_y) × 100

Where L_i(s) = number of aligned residues with similar/identical characteristics, L_y = length of the shorter sequence.

Key Concept — Optimal Alignment: The overall goal of pairwise sequence alignment is to find the best possible pairing of two sequences. One sequence must be shifted relative to the other to find the position where the maximum number of matches occur.
03 — Gaps & Penalties

Inserting Gaps and Scoring Penalties

Gaps (insertions/deletions) are necessary to improve alignment quality. They represent biological insertions or deletions (indels) that occurred during evolution. However, gaps must not be inserted arbitrarily — a scoring scheme rewards matches and penalises mismatches and gaps.

AFRICA
A-R-CA   ← Global alignment with gaps inserted
Maximises base-to-base matches. Order maintained. Gap-to-gap matches not allowed.

For sequences GATTCGGACCTCGGATCCCGT and ATCGACTCGATCGT there are approximately 2³⁹ possible alignments — making exhaustive evaluation impossible for long sequences.

Constant Gap Penalty

A fixed penalty "-a" for any gap, regardless of length. Minimises the total number of gaps but treats a 1-base gap the same as a 10-base gap.

Linear Gap Penalty

A penalty of "-a" per unit length of a gap. The penalty for one large gap equals the same as for many small gaps of equal total length.

Affine Gap Penalty ⭐

Differential penalty for opening a gap ("-y") vs extending it ("-z"). Total = y + (k–1)z where k = gap length. Biologically realistic: one long gap is more likely than many small ones.

⚠️ Affine Formula: G = y + (k–1)z  |  Example: gap-open = -5, gap-extend = -1 → a gap of 3 bases costs: 5 + (3-1)×1 = -7. Terminal gaps are often treated with no penalty because many truly homologous sequences are of different lengths.
04 — Algorithms Overview

Types of Alignment Algorithms

Exhaustive (Optimal) Methods

  • Produce a complete list of all possible alignments
  • Score and select the alignment with the best score
  • Guaranteed to find the OPTIMAL alignment
  • Dynamic programming (Needleman-Wunsch 1970)
  • Not practical for large database searching
  • Not practical for multiple sequence alignment

Heuristic Methods

  • Break sequence into short "words" (k-mers)
  • Use approximations to speed up comparisons
  • Find a solution close to the best one, fast
  • Small risk that the true alignment is missed
  • Examples: BLAST, ClustalW, Tcoffee
  • Subtypes: Progressive, Iterative, Block-based
🔄

Progressive Alignment

Aligns the most closely related pair first, then adds the next most similar sequence, following the "once a gap, always a gap" rule. Used by ClustalW.

🔁

Iterative Alignment

Realigns the initial sequences and adds new sequences iteratively to the growing multiple sequence alignment, improving on progressive alignment.

📦

Block-Based Alignment

The basic form of heuristic alignment methods. Identifies blocks of locally similar regions and connects them into a global alignment path.

⚡

Dynamic Programming

Exhaustive optimal method. Breaks the problem into smaller subproblems, solves each optimally, and constructs the globally optimal solution. Foundation of NW and SW algorithms.

05 — Global Alignment

Needleman-Wunsch — Global Alignment

Global alignment assumes the two sequences are generally similar over their entire length. Alignment runs from beginning to end of both sequences. Most applicable for two closely related sequences of roughly the same length.

Needleman-Wunsch (1970)

A dynamic programming algorithm that guarantees the optimal global alignment. Any partial subpath along the optimal path must itself be the optimal path to that point — the key dynamic programming property.

Scoring Scheme

Match = +1 (reward), Mismatch = 0 or penalty (punish), Gap = -1 (punish). The alignment with the highest total score is the optimal alignment.

Disadvantage

May fail for divergent sequences or sequences of very different lengths. It forces alignment across the entire length, which can produce poor results for sequences sharing only local regions of similarity.

Dynamic Algorithm — 3 Steps
1️⃣

Initialisation

Create a matrix with M+2 columns and N+2 rows. Fill the second row and column with 0, -1, -2, -3... (gap penalties).

2️⃣

Score Calculation

Fill each cell by taking the maximum of: diagonal (match/mismatch) + score, left (gap) - 1, or above (gap) - 1. Record the traceback direction in each cell.

3️⃣

Traceback

Start from the bottom-right cell (highest score). Follow traceback arrows (diagonal = match/mismatch, left/up = gap) back to the top-left. The path gives the optimal alignment.

📊 Example — AGATCAT vs AGCGT (match +1, mismatch 0, gap -1):
Optimal global alignment score = 2
A G A T C A T
A G - - C G T
Score: 1+1+(-1)+(-1)+1+0+1 = 2
06 — Local & Semi-global

Smith-Waterman — Local Alignment

Local Alignment

Finds the local regions with the highest level of similarity between two sequences and aligns only those regions. More appropriate for divergent biological sequences containing conserved domains or motifs. The two sequences can be of different lengths.

Semi-global Alignment

Looks for the best alignment between a short and a long sequence. Terminal gaps are scored differently from internal gaps because they often arise from differences in sequence length, not true indels.

Smith-Waterman Algorithm — Key Features
Initialise with Zeros

First row and column set to zero (unlike NW which uses gap penalties). This allows the alignment to start anywhere in the matrix.

Fourth Move Option

In addition to diagonal, left, and up moves, there is a fourth option: score zero if all other movements would give a negative score. This allows paths to restart.

Multiple Paths

When score drops to zero, the current path ends and a new one can begin. Many paths may exist; the one with the highest score is reported as the optimal local alignment.

Traceback Difference

Start traceback from the maximum score anywhere in the matrix (not the corner) and work backward until reaching a cell with score zero.

📊 Example — AGATCAT vs AGA (match +1, mismatch 0, gap -1):
Optimal LOCAL alignment score = 3
A G A T C A T
A G A
Score: 1+1+1+0+0+0+0 = 3  (only the matching local region is reported)
07 — Dot Matrix

Dot Matrix — Visualising Sequence Relationships

The dot matrix is the most basic sequence alignment visualisation method. It uses the two sequences as coordinates of a 2D graph and plots points wherever the sequences match.

How It Works

Sequence 1 on the X-axis, Sequence 2 on the Y-axis. A dot is placed wherever there is a match. When sequences match consistently over a region, dots merge into a diagonal line segment.

Advantages

Easy identification of the regions of greatest similarity. Reveals complex relationships involving multiple regions of local similarity. Identifies sequence repeat regions as parallel diagonals. Useful for chromosomal repeat detection.

Disadvantages

Lacks statistical rigour — cannot assess the quality of the alignment. Restricted to pairwise alignment only. Difficult to scale up to multiple sequence alignment.

📊 Reading a Dot Matrix: A continuous diagonal line = high similarity region. Breaks in the diagonal = gaps/indels. Parallel diagonals = repeated sequences. Off-diagonal clusters = inverted repeats or mismatches.
08 — Summary

What You Should Remember

  • Sequence alignment is the fundamental method for comparing DNA, RNA, and protein sequences to find regions of similarity.
  • Percentage identity/similarity can be calculated using specific formulas for equal-length and unequal-length sequences.
  • Gaps (indels) must be inserted with penalty schemes — constant, linear, or affine (most biologically realistic).
  • The affine gap penalty G = y + (k-1)z favours one long gap over many short ones, reflecting biological reality.
  • Exhaustive (dynamic programming) methods guarantee optimal alignment; heuristic methods trade optimality for speed.
  • Needleman-Wunsch (1970) performs global alignment — best for sequences of similar length and overall similarity.
  • Smith-Waterman performs local alignment — best for divergent sequences or finding conserved domains and motifs.
  • Semi-global alignment handles a short query against a long sequence with no terminal gap penalties.
  • The dot matrix provides a visual, parameter-free way to compare two sequences and detect repeats and inversions.
