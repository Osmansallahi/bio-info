01 — Introduction

What Are Biological Databases?

The field of bioinformatics relies on a vast ecosystem of online databases and resources. A researcher must know which databases exist, what tools are available to mine them, and how to search across resources efficiently.

🔓

Public or Private

Databases may be openly accessible or require registration/permission. This determines both who can access the data and who can submit to it.

🧬

Data Types

They store protein sequences, nucleotide sequences, 3D structures, literature references, and functional annotations, among many other data types.

🎯

Scope

A database can be generalised (covering many organisms and data types) or specialised (focused on a specific disease, organism, or method).

✅

Curation

Curated databases have expert-reviewed, high-quality entries. Non-curated databases accept submissions automatically, trading quality for volume.

02 — Database Types

Primary vs Secondary Databases

🥇

Primary Databases

Contain the original raw sequence data as submitted by researchers. They are the starting point for all biological sequence information.

Examples: GenBank (NCBI), EMBL (EBI), DDBJ — all part of INSDC.

🥈

Secondary Databases

Built on top of primary data. They provide in-depth annotation, analysis, and derived information — adding biological meaning beyond raw sequences.

Examples: UniProt, RefSeq, Ensembl, PDB.

INSDC — The International Nucleotide Sequence Database Collaboration

Genomic sequence data is shared across three public databases that synchronise their data daily. Each has its own accession numbers, tools, and submission system.

03 — NCBI

National Centre for Biotechnology Information

🏛️

What is NCBI?

A National Institute of Health (NIH) funded initiative established to store and disseminate molecular biology information. It has grown dramatically since the completion of the Human Genome Project and the reduction in sequencing costs.

🧱

GenBank

The NIH genetic sequence database — an annotated collection of all publicly available DNA sequences. It is part of INSDC and is updated approximately every two months.

GenBank Divisions
Complete Microbial Genomes

Full genome sequences from microorganisms.

WGS — Whole Genome Shotgun

Large-scale random sequencing of entire genomes.

TSA — Transcriptome Shotgun Assembly

Assembled mRNA/cDNA sequences from transcriptomics experiments.

HTGs — High-throughput Genomic Sequences

Unfinished genome sequences from large-scale projects.

TLS — Targeted Locus Study

Sequences from targeted sequencing of specific gene regions.

TPA — Third Party Annotation

Entries assembled from existing sequences with new annotation.

04 — EMBL-EBI

European Bioinformatics Institute

EMBL-EBI maintains the world's most comprehensive range of freely available and up-to-date molecular databases. It also offers both online and live training events for using their resources.

🔮

AlphaFold DB

Database of protein structure predictions for numerous species, powered by DeepMind's AlphaFold AI model.

📊

ArrayExpress

A database of functional genomics experiments including microarray and RNAseq expression data, typically linked to publications.

🖼️

BioImage Archive

EMBL-EBI's general purpose image archive, accepting molecular biology imaging data associated with peer-reviewed publications.

🧬

Ensembl

A resource for high-quality integrated genome annotation data — browse genes, transcripts, and regulatory features across many species.

🔗

UniProt

Universal Protein Resource — the leading database for protein sequence and functional annotation data.

🏗️

PDBe

Protein Data Bank Europe — collection of experimentally determined 3D structural data of biological macromolecules.

🏷️

InterPro

Database of protein families, domains, and conserved sites — integrating data from multiple member databases.

05 — Accessing Data

Why and How to Access Sequence Data

Common reasons a researcher needs to access sequence data:

Know functional region sequences

Identify the exact sequence of a region known to have a biological function (e.g., a promoter or coding sequence).

Identify variants

Find nucleotide changes or mutations relative to a reference sequence.

Comparative analysis

Compare your sequence of interest to those previously identified and deposited by others.

Disease associations

Find diseases associated with variation in a specific gene of interest.

Retrieve published datasets

Access raw sequencing data from a previously published study for re-analysis.

Key principles for efficient data retrieval:

Be specific about your query

It is critical to be clear about what data type you are looking for before starting a search. Different tools behave differently depending on query type.

Use Portal Search

Both NCBI and EBI provide portal search pages that let you search across all available databases with a single query, returning categorised results.

Tools link annotations

Most tools have been developed to link to all related annotations for a given query — genes link to proteins, to literature, to clinical data, and so on.

06 — Popular Databases

Key Databases to Know

Gene

One-stop resource for all annotation information about a gene — sequence, expression, phenotypes, variants, literature, pathways, and more.

PubMed

Extensive biomedical literature database — search millions of abstracts and full-text articles in the life sciences.

Nucleotide

Database of all DNA sequence data deposited at NCBI — includes GenBank, RefSeq, and other sources.

dbSNP

Database of single nucleotide polymorphisms and other small genetic variants in humans and other organisms.

Protein

Database of protein sequences from GenBank CDS translations, RefSeq, UniProt/Swiss-Prot, and other sources.

RefSeq

Comprehensive, integrated, well-annotated set of reference sequences — genomic, transcript, and protein — maintained by NCBI.

OMIM

Online Mendelian Inheritance in Man — database of human genes and genetic phenotypes with clinical descriptions.

ClinVar

Database of genomic variants and their relationship to human health — links variants to clinical significance and supporting evidence.

07 — Accession Numbers

Reading Accession Number Prefixes

Every GenBank record — consisting of both a sequence and its annotations — is assigned a unique identifier called an accession number. The prefix tells you what kind of sequence it is.

NC_ / NG_
Known RefSeq
Genomic regions or complete assembly (chromosomes, organelles).
NM_
Known RefSeq
Experimentally confirmed mRNA transcript sequences.
NR_
Known RefSeq
Non-coding RNA sequences (lncRNA, miRNA, rRNA, etc.).
NP_
Known RefSeq
Protein sequences derived from known RefSeq mRNAs.
NT_ / NW_
Known RefSeq
Genomic contigs or scaffolds — unfinished assembly pieces.
XM_
Model RefSeq
Computationally predicted mRNA — not experimentally confirmed.
XR_
Model RefSeq
Computationally predicted non-coding RNA.
XP_
Model RefSeq
Computationally predicted protein — derived from model mRNA.

💡 Rule of thumb: N prefix = known/reviewed  ·  X prefix = predicted/model
Second letter: C/G = genomic  ·  M = mRNA  ·  R = RNA  ·  P = protein  ·  T/W = contig/scaffold

08 — Specialised Databases

Beyond NCBI & EBI — Specialised Resources

A large number of specialised databases exist for specific organisms, diseases, or data types. Most of their sequences are also in GenBank/EMBL/DDBJ, but they additionally integrate data not found elsewhere (e.g., clinical information + genetic data) and offer dedicated analysis and visualisation tools.

🦟

PlasmoDB

Plasmodium (malaria parasite) genomics — VEuPathDB project.

🧫

Sanger Institute

1000 Genomes, Cancer Gene Census, bacterial & bacteriophage data.

🦠

GISAID

Global initiative for sharing all influenza data — extended to SARS-CoV-2. Over 16 million sequences shared.

📈

GWAS Catalog

NHGRI-EBI catalogue of human genome-wide association studies (GWAS).

🔒

EGA

European Genome-phenome Archive — permanent archive for sensitive human genetic & phenotypic data requiring controlled access.

09 — Summary

What You Should Remember

  • Databases store different types of biological data — nucleotides, proteins, structures, literature, and clinical information.
  • Primary databases store raw sequence data as submitted; secondary databases add expert annotation and derived information.
  • The INSDC links NCBI (GenBank), EMBL-EBI, and DDBJ — all three synchronise their sequence data daily.
  • NCBI and EBI are the two most popular bioinformatics resource hubs, each containing dozens of databases.
  • Knowledge of which databases exist makes it far easier to find specific information for your research.
  • Portal search tools at NCBI and EBI allow you to query all available databases simultaneously with a single search term.
  • Accession number prefixes encode the type and status (known vs. predicted) of a sequence record.
  • Specialised databases are invaluable for organism/disease-specific data and often integrate clinical context not found elsewhere.
